In his entertaining [The Manipulation of Choice: Ethics and Libertarian Paternalism](https://doi.org/10.1057/9781137313577), Mark White provides a three-pronged attack on nudging. The behavioural economist's focus on preferences is too narrow. We cannot know someone's true interests. And even if there was a way to discern their interests, we have no right to interfere with their choices to make them better.

While I disagree with the first of these - White's claim that choice is more than preferences (which I'll cover in more detail at the end) - White's case for the latter two doesn't rely on it.

Instead, his case for our inability to discern someone's beliefs largely relies on (sensible) thought experiments. White opens his argument by picturing a heavyset man eating a muffin in a bakery. On first sight, we might think he should have strolled to a fruit shop instead. But why is he eating the muffin? Re-enacting a tradition he used to do with his recently deceased grandfather? Celebrating the weight loss from a year without muffins? To spite his wife? To sample for a cafe review he is writing? And so on. We can observe the behaviour but can't know the intention behind it. Once we acknowledge that point, we can either grant the person the benefit of the doubt and assume the choice is in their best interests, or we can make assumptions about what those interests are.

I am sympathetic to this critique. Life is more than a monk-ish march to a late but boring death. Instead, it might involve extremes, choices with trade-offs and a lot of choices that someone else - or even yourself at another time - wouldn't make.

Absent knowledge of people's interests, White argues that policy makers tend to substitute their own preferences. I agree that this is often the case, although White does not steelman this argument. Throughout Thaler and Sunstein's Nudge - the book that serves White's major foil - they refer to the standard of "as judged by themselves". This could come from later assessments of whether a person is happy with the choice. Do they regret it? If happy, the nudge passes the test. Alternatively, we could observe what happens when barriers such as time pressure and confusion are removed. White never mentions this standard. However, in fairness to White, it's rare to see policymakers reference it either.

That lack of steelmanning is apparent in other parts of the book. For example, he doesn't spend much effort looking for examples where it is a fair assumption what that person's preferences are. Think of "nudges" in the form of reminders about an upcoming court date to increase court attendance. I suggest it's a fair assumption that someone would prefer to actively decide to not attend rather than forget. Similarly, assuming people want more money rather than less (achieved by, say, opting them into a government rebate) seems fair.

Absent this steelmanning, however, White's critiques still bite. It is rare to see nudges rolled out with such an unambiguous judgement as to what is in someone's interest. Read through the list of reports by various nudge units and you can almost always imagine people being nudged in a way that does not match their interests.

At this point Thaler and Sunstein would argue that given a nudge does not remove choices, those people can simply choose in their interests. The nudge is voluntary. But it is here that White turns to his argument on what voluntary actually means. A decision is not voluntary if it is because of manipulation. It is not voluntary where circumstances are distorting.

The final limb of White's argument - that even if we knew someone's preferences we should still leave them alone - is largely a traditional critique of paternalism. Combined with his view on the "voluntary" nature of nudges, "libertarian paternalism" is seen as least as bad (if not more so) than traditional paternalism such as taxes and bans. At least hard paternalism is transparent.

As seems to be the case for most books on nudges, the closing chapter involves some rose-tinted glasses about human decision making. White argues for presenting information neutrally, as you might see with credit card or mortgage product disclosure. He doesn't note that no-one reads or accesses them. He suggests holding people accountable and responsible for their choices (e.g. costlier insurance if you're tubby), without tackling the question that we already bear a lot of the costs (e.g. dying much earlier with a much lower quality of life) yet still engage in those behaviours. He also argues that nudges deprive people of the ability to learn, despite the lack of evidence in learning in many domains. Further, one argument for nudges is that they are often applied to either one-off decisions (e.g. a major purchase) or to decisions where the consequences are felt long after the choice is made (e.g. retirement savings), preventing learning.

That's not to say that any of these are bad ideas. It's just that they're not going to shift the dial much on decision making quality.

White also suggests we should help people overcome their cognitive biases and heuristics. Although not referenced directly, this thread has some similarities to the nudge versus boost debates. How can we improve decision making? Maybe its through creating more time (e.g. cooling off periods). However, even here the rose-coloured glasses reappear. Perhaps by consumer demand we'll end up with information presentation from merchants and lenders that enable easier comparison? The evidence out there doesn't provide much support.

Putting it together, White builds a solid case. His critique of understanding a person's preferences lacks the technical strength of Robert Sugden's (excellent) [The Community of Advantage](/posts/robert-sugdens-the-community-of-advantage-a-behavioural-economists-defence-of-the-market.md), but he turns it into a more full-throated critique of nudging.

Before closing, I want to turn to the point of disagreement I flagged on Whites first two chapters. White argues that preferences do not include "principles", such as the moral principles of honesty and courage. Principles captures the idea that a person may not want to buy a car made by a company by poor labour practices or from a car dealer who has made racist comments. These principles can't be measured.

White provides the following example:

> Jodi normally gives $100 each month to her local animal shelter out of her love and sympathy for abandoned pets. If this choice were based on a preference, we would say that she chose to donate the $100 because the value of that donation to her is greater than its opportunity cost (the value of whatever else she would do with the money). If Jodi’s circumstances changed and she wants (or needs) to use the money for something else—or simply has less to give—that trade-off may no longer be attractive to her. She might give only $75 to the shelter next month, because she now has more pressing uses for the other $25. Of course, the opposite may also happen instead, where she has less need for her last bit of money (or more money to use to satisfy her preferences), and so she might increase her donation to $150. As the opportunity cost of her donation changes, so does the best trade-off she can make, and her donation would change with it.
>
>But now let’s say Jodi promised to give $100 to the animal shelter each month. Her promise is a commitment, which is supported by a principle—specifically, a moral principle—to honor commitments, and possibly also by a principle that motivated the promise in the first place. In this case, when Jodi’s circumstances change, she will be less likely to lower her donation. To maintain her commitment, she will try to find other ways to meet her changing wants or needs without compromising her promise to the shelter. Her promised donation, which she knows the shelter counts on each month, is not something she is willing to adjust slightly in response to an increase in the opportunity cost of giving. Her promise, and the commitment behind it, takes this aspect of choice to a different level. This is not to say that there is no circumstance that would lead Jodi to break or compromise her promise: she might lose her job, for instance, or find another cause that she feels serves abandoned animals better. Commitments are not absolute, but neither are they subject to the small changes and adjustments that preferences are, wherein we devote a few more resources to this and a few less to that when our circumstances change. It will take more than a slight change in the opportunity cost of giving to make Jodi break her promise to the shelter, just as it would take a lot to make most of us break promises—that’s what makes them promises.

I struggle to see how Jodi's desire to hold to the promise can't be captured by preferences. There's nothing about preferences to say that a slight change of the opportunity cost would cause her to break her promise (although I would predict that at some point she would). Preferences can have many shapes. The marginal rate of substitution could be very low. Preferences can be lexiographic. The demand curve might be vertical. Whatever.

White also argues that economists equate preference satisfaction with wellbeing.

Welfare economics - needs a common basis - $

But value anything - WTP etc (although can critique these.)

In making this case, White displays a lack of engagement with the economic literature. He states that for economists "everything is reducible to a numerical value", despite basic consumer choice analysis being ordinal rather than cardinal. 

Having said all this, classifying "principles" as preferences or otherwise is beside the point, and none of the latter arguments hang on this.
