White argues that the nudgers have no way of knowing someone's true interests, and even if there was a way to discern this, they have no right to interfere in their choices to make them better.

While I am sympathetic to both of those arguments, I disagreed with White's starting point in the first two chapters 

My major disagreement with White comes from his belief that "choice is more than preferences". He argues that preferences do not include "principles", such as the moral principles of honesty and courage. Principles captures the idea that a person may not want to buy a car made by a company by poor labour practices or from a car dealer who has made racist comments. These principles can't be measured.

I disagree both that these are different from preferences and that they can't be measured.

Even though she hasn't changed her decision, the opportunity cost has changed. And there is quite possibly a point where it breaks. But even if it doesn't - there's nothing about the shape of preferences that require a point where the decision flips.

Having said all this, classifying "principles" as preferences or otherwise is beside the point, and none of the latter arguments hang on this.

White opens his chapter on out inability to determine a person's true interests by picturing a heavyset man eating a muffin in a bakery. On first sight, we might think he should have strolled to a fruit shop instead. But why is he eating the muffin? Re-enacting a tradition he used to do with his recently deceased grandfather? Celebrating the weight loss from a year without muffins? To spite his wife? To sample for a cafe review he is writing? And so on. We can't know. We can observe the behaviour but can't know the intention behind it.

Once we acknowledge that point, we can either grant the person the benefit of the doubt and assume the choice is in their best interests, or we can make assumptions about what those interests are. If the latter, what assumptions should we make?
