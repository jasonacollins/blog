In his entertaining [The Manipulation of Choice: Ethics and Libertarian Paternalism](https://doi.org/10.1057/9781137313577), Mark White provides a three-pronged attack on nudging. The behavioural economist's focus on preferences is too narrow. We cannot know someone's true interests. And even if there was a way to discern their interests, we have no right to interfere with their choices to make them better.

While I disagree with the first of these - White's claim that choice is more than preferences (which I'll cover in more detail at the end) - White's case for the latter two doesn't rely on it.

Instead, his case for our inability to discern someone's beliefs largely relies on (sensible) thought experiments. White opens his argument by picturing a heavyset man eating a muffin in a bakery. On first sight, we might think he should have strolled to a fruit shop instead. But why is he eating the muffin? Re-enacting a tradition he used to do with his recently deceased grandfather? Celebrating the weight loss from a year without muffins? To spite his wife? To sample for a cafe review he is writing? And so on. We can't know. We can observe the behaviour but can't know the intention behind it. Once we acknowledge that point, we can either grant the person the benefit of the doubt and assume the choice is in their best interests, or we can make assumptions about what those interests are.

I am sympathetic to this critique. Life is more than a monk-ish march to a late but boring death. Instead, it might involve extremes, choices with trade-offs and a lot of choices that someone else - or even yourself at another time - wouldn't make.

White argues that, absent that knowledge, policy makers tend to sustitute their own preferences..

One limb of White's critique is the nudges deprive people of the ability to learn. However, one argument for nudges is that they are often applied to either one-off decisions or to decisions where the consequences are felt long after the choice is made.



Through the book, White rarely steelmans his arguments. For example, when claiming that we cannot know someone else's preferences, he doesn't spend much effort looking for examples where it is a fair assumption what that person's preferences are. Think of "nudges" in the form of reminders about an upcoming court date or presenting credit card payment information so that you know how long it would take to pay off your credit card if you continued to make minimum payments. Information doesn't necessarily have to be presented in a way to capitalise on mental failings, but rather help overcome them.

Another potential steelman omitted by White is the idea of "as judged by themselves", a critical element of Thaler and Sunstein's book.

He might accept the the court reminders as a presentation of neutral information, something that he supports. But when he does this, he can't seem to help drifting back to a view of people who make good decisions if the information is (somewhere) available to them.

Finally, to the point of disagreement which forms the bulk of the first two chapters. White argues that preferences do not include "principles", such as the moral principles of honesty and courage. Principles captures the idea that a person may not want to buy a car made by a company by poor labour practices or from a car dealer who has made racist comments. These principles can't be measured.
