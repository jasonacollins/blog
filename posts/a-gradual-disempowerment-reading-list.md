I recently completed BlueDot Impact's [AGI Strategy Course](https://bluedot.org/courses/agi-strategy). (I recommend it.) Through the weekly discussions I went down the rabbit hole of [gradual disempowerment](https://gradual-disempowerment.ai/). I find many AI threats compelling, but gradual disempowerment is something where my skills and work perhaps offer the most insight.

One follow up action from the course was a Gradual Disempowerment reading list.

The following list reflects my worldview.

I have written in the past about how more decisions should be handed to AI (e.g. here, here and here). Since Paul Meehl's Clinical Versus Statistical Prediction (Meehl, P. E. (1954). *Clinical versus statistical prediction: A theoretical analysis and a review of the evidence.* University of Minnesota Press. [https://doi.org/10.1037/11281-000](https://psycnet.apa.org/doi/10.1037/11281-000))was published in 1954, there has been a rapidly accumulating body of research of outperformance in certain domains.

While under-reliance was my past focus, I can see an endpoint where this doesn't end well. This is perhaps best captured by the idea of gradual disempowerment, where competitive pressures between companies and states drives the adoption of AI decision making to the extent where humans slowly exit the loop. The result is a disempowered humanity. This handover also magnifies risks from misaligned AI or rogue actors able to affect these AI decisions.

The below reading list contains some of the core readings relating to gradual disempowerment, along with two bodies of research that I believe add an interesting

The first relates to how we under- or over-rely on automation and AI. This literature spans over 40 years and provides great insights into how humans handover control.

The second relates to deskilling and the erosion of human capability. As we handover to AI, we may no longer be able to be a useful participants in processes that we used to control.

You can see a flavour of my current work under some of the headings.

## The core thesis

@kulveit2025 [Gradual Disempowerment: Systemic Existential Risks from Incremental AI Development](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.16946): The anchor paper.

@chakravorty2025 [Gradual Disempowerment Summary](https://blog.bluedot.org/p/gradual-disempowerment-summary): BlueDot Impact's summary of the Kulveit et. al. paper. A quick way to get across it.

@drago2025 [The Intelligence Curse](https://intelligence-curse.ai/) ([pdf](https://intelligence-curse.ai/intelligence-curse.pdf)): If states and companies don't need people, they won't care about them. Chapters 2 and 3 are particularly relevant to the gradual disempowerment thesis.

@fenwick2025 [Gradual disempowerment](https://80000hours.org/problem-profiles/gradual-disempowerment/): A summary of the Gradual Disempowerment thesis on 80,000 hours. Includes links to other readings.

@wiblin2026 [#234 – David Duvenaud on why ‘aligned AI’ could still kill democracy](https://80000hours.org/podcast/episodes/david-duvenaud-gradual-disempowerment/): Robert Wiblin interview David Duvenaud, one of the authors of @kulveit2025.
